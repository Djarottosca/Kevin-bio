Age=Maturity=(?)

A quote from a novelist, Lawana Blackwell "Age is no guarantee of maturity" so maturity is in attitude and behaviour. Many people who are older but still behave childishly, are still immature.

This article offers a philosophical examination of human maturity. The argument is that maturity in the human world has an infinite structure because it demands responsibility for the world. The world is a product of constitution: human beings produce the world, which is functionally a level of meaningfulness; but as each human being constitutes the world, each human being also enters the constituted world. Maturity thus demands responsibility not only for what is produced through one's agency but also what precedes one's agency. The structure of the conceived world is such that it can never be fully complete. Therefore, the responsibilities that exist in this world are unlimited rather than finite.

This notion of maturity as infinite responsibility is examined through an enquiry into four questions. The first three questions pertain to maturity in the domains of human reason, action, and sciences. Mature reason is said to involve the development of critical responsibility. This implies the responsibility to expand the range of evidence evaluated and to expand the means of critical assessment of evidence, which requires efforts that go beyond rationality. Mature action, in turn, must take into account the ambiguity of actions and intentions, as these meanings in the human world are never fully formed. This requires a critical effort to distinguish needs and desires to establish intersubjectively meaningful criteria by which one can be held accountable. Human sciences, then, are called upon to examine human "needs" apart from the fundamental contingencies of human life. This requires mature human sciences to reject deterministic metaphysics in favour of a relational conception of human reality. The fourth and final question raised is how maturity can be chosen. Maturity is understood as the result of various human acts of judgement, so it is not the result of individual efforts towards personal maturity, but is produced through political agency to judge maturity.

    One of the characteristics is self-awareness and self-knowledge; Understanding the position and can place themselves according to their position.

So what is maturity? Actually, maturity is a quality, or characteristic that consists of many things, such as, for example: Compassion, Kindness, Consideration, Balance or Inner Peace, Wisdom, Prudence, Perspective, Practicality, Honesty, perhaps a proper awe of existence, and what some call "Fortitude" - the ability to persevere in the face of adversity, the ability to endure hardship without constantly complaining or feeling victimised. A mature person is not quick to anger. Such a person is not careless with actions, thoughts, or feelings. Maturity rises to the level of appreciation and gratitude for all that is good and beautiful in life, while accepting that there are limitations and imperfections in the world. Mature people may want and work hard to change and improve things around them, but they will not wallow in aggravation, resentment, and frustration about things that need to be changed.

For most of history, in most civilisations and societies, people regarded maturity as something to aspire to, hope for, and respect. In the past, people often tried to act more mature than their age would suggest. This still happens sometimes, but in very limited contexts, such as when someone is trying to get their first job. Immaturity, on the other hand, now seems to almost rule the culture. We see many people acting less mature than what is expected of their age.

The ancient philosopher Diogenes was said to walk around everywhere during the daytime, carrying a lighted lamp, or lantern. Asked what he was doing, he liked to say, "Looking for an honest man". In our own time, he would have nearly as endless a trek looking for real maturity.

Immaturity is wrapped up in ego, a sense of entitlement, a lack of responsibility, and a tendency towards anger, as well as an inclination to delight in the flaws and sufferings of others. Immature people are prone to whining, resenting, and feeling slighted when others aren't suitably celebrating their specialness. Immature people throw fits and tantrums, regardless of age. They also tend to be as callous towards others as they are fragile in their own sense of need. And it makes you wonder how we ever got so far away from an appreciation of what it is to be truly mature.

Maturity is about proper growth and exemplary health. We should encourage it in others and seek to enhance it in our own lives.

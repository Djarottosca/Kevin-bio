Petrol vs Diesel Engines

Diesel and gasoline engines are the two most commonly used internal combustion engines, although their operations seem similar they have some interesting differences and each has advantages when compared to the other. This article was created to explore these differences and advantages from a scientific point of view.

The majority of both diesel and gasoline engines use a 4-stroke cycle of suction, compression, stroke, and exhaust. However, diesel engines often have a higher compression ratio than gasoline engines. The main components of diesel engines and gasoline engines are similar, such as pistons, cylinders, suction, exhaust, and crankshaft valves. Although there are differences in compression ratio and ignition system, the structure and operating principle of these components are basically the same. Both produce mechanical power.

There is a difference between these two engines due to the way the fuel is burned. Diesel oil is not volatile, its boiling point is 250-400°Celsius, and it does not mix well with air. When atomized diesel fuel is sprayed into a room with high temperature air, spontaneous combustion will occur. On the other hand, gasoline is volatile, the boiling point of gasoline is 40-200°Celsius, so it mixes with air efficiently, the result is that a spark is enough to produce smooth combustion. This indicates that in a gasoline engine, the fuel and air must be mixed first while in a diesel engine the mixing only occurs during combustion. This is why diesel engines use injectors while gasoline engines use spark plugs.

In a diesel engine, combustion can start anywhere in the combustion chamber and the process is not controlled. Therefore, diesel engines are noisy and have a loud vibration, to reduce noise and excessive vibration problems, diesel engines require a more robust structural design than gasoline engines. Gasoline engines are less noisy and do not cause excessive vibration when compared to diesel engines, because the combustion process in the premixed mixture occurs smoothly and propagates well, this is why gasoline engines are always preferred by many people for light load use.

A diesel engine compresses only air, it can achieve a good compression ratio without the risk of self-ignition, but such a high compression ratio is not possible in a premixed gasoline engine. The higher the compression ratio the better the cycle efficiency is the reason why diesel engines have better fuel economy compared to gasoline engines.

    Depending on the type of gasoline or diesel engine, the desired properties of fuels vary. The most important properties of gasoline fuels are properties such as volatility and knocking resistance, whereas diesel fuels are required to have important fuel properties such as viscosity, surface tension, and ignition tendency. In gasoline fuels, volatility and knock resistance are one of the most important parameters affecting engine performance. The volatility of gasoline fuel affects the rate and amount of evaporation of the fuel in the intake channel and in the cylinder. The low volatility of the fuel influences the formation of sufficient air-fuel mixture, but when it is very volatile, it can prevent the flow of fuel by creating vapor bubbles in the suction channel with the local temperature increases. When the flame front advances during combustion, with the increasing temperature and pressure inside the cylinder, it compresses the air-fuel fill which the flame front cannot yet reach. Thus, the fuel can constitute another combustion front due to the fuel spontane. ously reaches the ignition temperature to heat and radiation. The combustion speeds of the flame fronts at these different points can be 300-350 m/s, and cylinder pressures may reciprocate to as high as 9-12 MPa. At these high speed and pressure values, the flame fronts are damped by hitting each other or against the walls of the combustion chamber. These damping are not only cause loss of energy but also increase the local heat conduction. As a result of this situation, engine performance decreases. This phenomenon is called a knock in gasoline engines and is an undesirable situation. The chemical structure of the fuel has a considerable effect on the autoignition temperature. Octane number (ON) is defined as the property of fuel to knocking resistance or how well the fuel itself ignites. The octane number is inversely proportional to the chain length of the fuel molecules. The shorter the molecular chain length of the fuels has, the higher the octane number is. However, the octane number is directly proportional to the branched side chain component. The higher the branching in the molecule chain leads to the higher octane number of the fuel. In other words, it causes higher knocking resistance of fuels. Generally, increasing the number of carbon atoms in the composition of the fuel has higher impact resistance. However, the octane numbers of cyclic molecules, naphthenes, alcohols, and aromatics are high.

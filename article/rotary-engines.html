Rotary Engine

The majority of both diesel and gasoline engines use a 4-stroke cycle of suction, compression, stroke, and exhaust. A rotary engine does the same four tasks, but each happens in a different part of the housing. It's like having a dedicated cylinder for each of the four jobs, with a piston that moves continuously from one to the next.

Rotary engines (originally designed and developed by Dr. Felix Wankel) are sometimes called Wankel engines, or Wankel rotary engines.

In this article, we will learn how a rotary engine works. Let's start with the basic workings.

Rotary engines, or Wankel engines, work on the basic principle of spinning rotation to generate power. Here is how a typical Wankel engine works:

  1. Working Chamber: A Wankel engine consists of a triangular working chamber bounded by the rotor, housing, and apex seals. The rotor is the main component that rotates inside the engine housing.

  2. Working Cycle: The Wankel engine goes through four stages in the working cycle: suction, compression, combustion, and exhaust.
  3. Suction: In this stage, the rotor rotates so that the volume of the working chamber enlarges and air mixed with fuel is sucked into the working chamber.
  4. Compression: After suction, the rotor rotates again, shrinking the volume of the working chamber and effectively compressing the air-fuel mixture.
  5. Combustion: When the air-fuel mixture is compressed, the spark plug ignites and causes rapid combustion of the mixture. The pressure generated by combustion pushes the rotor in the direction of rotation.
  6. Exhaust: After combustion, the rotor continues to rotate, pushing the exhaust gases out of the working chamber through the exhaust ducts.

This process continues to repeat continuously with the rotation of the rotor, producing continuous power.

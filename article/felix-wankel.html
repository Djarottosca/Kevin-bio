Felix Wankel

 

Felix Wankel, (born August 13, 1902, Lahr, Germany-died October 9, 1988, Lindau, Germany), German engineer and inventor of the Wankel rotary engine. Wankel reportedly invented the basic idea for a new type of internal combustion gasoline engine when he was only 17 years old. In 1924, Wankel established a small laboratory where he began the research and development of his dream engine, which would be able to achieve intake, compression, combustion, and exhaust, all while rotating. He carried his knowledge of rotary valves into his work at the German Aeronautical Research Establishment during World War II, and at the leading German motorcycle company, NSU Motorenwerk AG, beginning in 1951. Wankel completed the design of his first rotary piston engine in 1954, and the first unit was tested in 1957.

Wankel never earned an engineering degree or obtained a driver's license. As the son of a forestry official in the Black Forest region of southern Germany, he grew up in difficult circumstances after his father was killed in World War I. As a young man, he was convinced that he could do it. As a young man, convinced that he could design a practical rotary engine (the concept was well known but usually considered unworkable), he set up a small engineering business in Heidelberg while supporting himself with other jobs such as selling books. He was briefly a member of the Nazi Party before it came to power. During the Nazi period and World War II, he lived in Lindau, on Lake Constance (near the border with Switzerland), where he worked on designs for seals, unconventional rotary valves, and rotary engines for automobile and aircraft engines. At various times, he worked for the automobile companies Daimler-Benz and BMW as well as the German air force.

At the end of the war, Wankel's workshop was dismantled by the Allied authorities, and in 1951 he began working in Lindau with the research department of the engine manufacturer, NSU Motorenwerk AG. He completed the first design of a rotary engine for NSU in 1954, and prototype units were tested in 1957 and 1958. In 1961, Mazda, a Japanese automobile company, contracted NSU to manufacture and develop Wankel engines in Japan. Rotary-engined Mazda cars were introduced to the Japanese market in the 1960s and to the American market in 1971. Wankel established a series of his own research sites in Lindau, where he continued to work under contract for various companies on the fundamental problems and future applications of rotary engines.

The Wankel engine has a very different structure to conventional reciprocating piston engines. Instead of having a piston that moves up and down inside a cylinder, the Wankel engine has an orbiting, triangular-shaped rotor that rotates inside an enclosed chamber. Each quarter turn of the rotor completes the expansion or compression of the gas in the chamber, so the four functions that characterize all internal combustion engines, intake, compression, expansion, and exhaust, can be performed in a single turn of the rotor. The only moving parts are the rotor and the output shaft. In theory, the advantages of this design include its light weight, few moving parts, compactness, low initial cost, fewer repairs required, and relatively smooth performance.
